They're big, they're complicated, they're unsupported, and they're generally full of security issues. So why not just can them?

Legacy systems, that is, older computer systems that are still in use, are a common headache for IT departments and organizations around the world. Whether it's banking systems responsible for billions of dollars in transactions, or strategic nuclear launch systems relying on floppy disks (**UPDATE:** Turns out AFGSC finally updated this in mid-2019), so many mission-critical systems around the world are reliant on systems built before many of their operators were even born.

So what does this mean, why do systems get to this point, and what can we do about them?

# Characteristics #

The thing that makes Legacy systems a problem is more than just their age, but rather how critical they are.

**Difficulty to integrate** older technologies means that they can't be easily adapted to work with newer systems. In the modern days where your fridge orders your groceries, and your doorbell can be answered from the other side of the world, the standalone monolith is a problem. They were usually never designed to interact directly with other systems, and often making them do so is very difficult

# Why so hard to replace? #

Biggest question out there, especially when you have critical systems failing in their purpose, and lack of will to replace them. In early 2014, Victoria Police identified failures of their IT systems, including their significantly legacy LEAP database, as part of a chain that lead to a death of an 11-year old boy at the hands of his violent father. Despite plans to push forward with replacing the system, the program was eventually dropped. Despite upgrades, these legacy systems remain in place to this day. The arguments of "government incompetence" or "not trying hard enough" are two big theories cited about the failure to update their ailing systems. But this is only part of the picture.

We rarely keep old systems around just because we haven't gotten around to replacing them. We often keep them around because it's just become "too hard" to do otherwise. It doesn't take a visionary to understand that something old only gets older. So what could be so hard that organizations continuously flounder in this field?

## Layers of Business Rules ##

The key function of any business IT system is to perform a task in line with the businesses rules. So the first step to building an application is to understand those rules. Find them, document them, codify them, develop them, and implement them. *Viola*, simple.

But as anyone who's worked in large organizations (government especially) knows, it's rarely that simple. There are exceptions to rules, exceptions to those exceptions, and manual overrides for those exceptions. There are also certain rules that can NEVER EVER be breached... except when authorized by two senior managers with job titles that don't really exist anymore.

Compound about two decades worth of those rules, and eventually you'll end up with a system that beautifully handles all of the business processes... even if nobody exactly knows what they are. "Just read the documentation", and "Then ask the people who know those rules" are solid points, but if the documentation doesn't exist, and people have either forgotten or left, what then? The response of "That's not good enough" might make you feel better, and it might be true. But it doesn't solve the problem.

The Commonwealth Department of Human Services blew $135m on a replacement for their child support system that was a (total failure)[https://www.zdnet.com/article/human-services-finally-reveals-details-of-bungled-child-support-it-project/]. Cuba, the original system, remains in use to this day.
