> *This was originally published as an internal editorial for Australian Red Cross Victorian Emergency Services workforce members. It has been edited and expanded upon in this publication*

Fires kill people. The Emergency Services stop fires from killing people. Simple equation, right? But it doesn't stop there.

Indirect impacts often kill substantially more people than the main event. While everyone is dealing with the primary event, an unseen toll can be exacted on those nowhere near the lights and sirens.

# Previous Events #

![Image](/assets/2020-11-18-indirect-impacts-of-emergencies/1.png)

In 2009, Black Saturday resulted in 173 fatalities from the fires. But the heatwave surrounding it killed up to 374 additional Victorians. For reference, Black Saturday itself had most of the state at 40°C+ (Hopetoun hit 48.8°C), humidity as low as 2%, and wind over 100kph.

Last summer across Australia (2019/20), air pollution was an ongoing killer. With 34 fatalities as a direct result of the fires, up to 445 additional people perished due to the pollution in the air. That's a full x13 times the amount from the fires.

Why the difference? Along with the relative sizes of the disaster, we have gotten **substantially** better at ensuring the primacy of life during fires in the intervening decade.

# Inequal Impacts #

![Image](/assets/2020-11-18-indirect-impacts-of-emergencies/2.jpg)

Not everyone is equally exposed to these risks either. People experiencing homelessness, persons with chronic illnesses, disabilities, in social isolation, and with low socioeconomic status, along with many other factors, are always worst hit by disasters. Whether fire, flood, or pandemic, an elderly person living on their own on an aged care pension will almost always have a tougher stint compared with a young couple with secure full-time work.

Impacts aren't just measured in fatalities or hospitalisations either. Economic impacts hurt inequally too. If I as a working professional were to lose $100 per week, that would be pretty unpleasant. But for others, that would be absolutely *life-destroying*. Unsurprisingly, psychological impacts follow in very complex ways.

Consider a heatwave, like the ones described around Black Saturday: For many of us, that means staying inside, cold showers, and sitting in front of a fan, or if we're lucky and can afford to run one, an air conditioner. For a person experiencing homelessness, that means constant need to seek cooled shelter during the blistering hot day. Many of those public places actively still to this day try and actively move along these people, even in hazardous temperatures. And if the temperature stays high overnight, and it can for days at a time, the human body is taxed to its limits.

The result? For one, a few bad nights sleep, and needing to stay inside. For the other, a constant fight for survival, and potentially life-threatening health impacts.

# Pandemic Pressure #

![Image](/assets/2020-11-18-indirect-impacts-of-emergencies/3.png)

Indirect impacts are super-topical with our current disaster of COVID-19. At time of writing, we've had over 54 million confirmed cases worldwide. But we know potentially billions of people have been impacted in some way. The health impacts alone are proving deep and complex, with the unravelling complexities of what's becoming know as "Post-COVID Syndrome".

Beyond that, many people have become more reluctant to seek medical care for existing conditions, with up to 41% of adults in the US reporting they are delaying care, including for urgent issues, due to the pandemic. We know that health isn't just physiological, but psychological and emotional. Skyrocketing unemployment, economic stresses, widespread fear, and restriction fatigue take their toll every day.

Many institutions have already begun to make estimates with figures of millions of lives, and trillions of dollars in impacts worldwide. Even so, it will take years to truly quantify the final tolls taken on humanity.

# Future Journeys #

![Image](/assets/2020-11-18-indirect-impacts-of-emergencies/4.jpg)

This isn't something most of us will deal with on a daily basis with Red Cross ES, and the Control Agencies for emergencies will be the ones telling us about impacts. All the same, it's worth understanding that bigger context, particularly thinking about the psychosocial impacts on people over the long-term into Recovery. For those of us attending MEMPC meetings during peacetime, or EMT's during activations, having a thought to these impacts is all the more critical.

We've gotten better at understanding these indirect impacts. People living across south-eastern Australia across the summer of 2019/20 would remember the oppressive air quality during the fires, even from hundreds of kilometres away. We had air quality alerts, and messages plastered all over the news to warn us. All the same, we as a sector still need to improve the way we treat emergencies as a complex collection of consequences, rather than "see the hazard, treat the hazard. 

The old saying goes "If there's smoke, there must be fire". So, why only be concerned with the fire?
